# How I Use AI to Build Websites Faster Without Cutting Corners

Every developer I know uses AI now. The ones who say they don't are either lying or writing code the slow way on principle. Both are fine. But neither is interesting.

What is interesting: the massive gap between what AI-powered web development actually looks like in practice versus what LinkedIn influencers want you to believe. One camp says you can vibe-code an entire production app in an afternoon. The other camp says AI writes garbage code and real developers don't need it. Both camps are wrong, and the truth is more useful than either take.

A decade of building web applications taught me what good looks like. Enterprise platforms at Bell Canada. Financial systems at Taulia before SAP acquired them. Consumer-facing work at Sofar Sounds and Wells Fargo. These days, through Tatham Tech, AI is in the workflow every single day. It makes the work faster. It does not make the work easier. There's a difference.

## Design Phase: AI Generates, I Curate

AI can spit out layout concepts and design directions in minutes. Feed it brand guidelines, competitor sites, and project goals. You get a stack of starting points before your coffee gets cold.

Starting points. Not finished designs.

AI design output gravitates toward safe and familiar because that is literally what it was trained on. Patterns it has seen before. The average of everything. Fine for brainstorming. Not enough for a site that needs to convert visitors or stand out from the twelve competitors who also used AI to generate their layouts.

The real work is knowing which concepts have potential and which are polished dead ends. That instinct comes from years of building interfaces real people actually use. A SaaS dashboard and a local restaurant landing page need fundamentally different things. AI doesn't make that distinction well on its own.

Keep what works. Kill what doesn't. Push the surviving concepts further than AI would go alone. The design phase takes less time but still produces original, strategic work.

## Code Generation: AI Writes Boilerplate, I Architect

This is where AI saves the most time. Also where the experience gap is widest.

Boilerplate code is AI's sweet spot. Component scaffolding, data fetching patterns, form validation, responsive grid layouts. The structural plumbing every project needs but nobody writes a conference talk about. AI handles it in seconds.

Architecture is a different story. AI does not know your e-commerce site needs to handle 10,000 concurrent users during a flash sale. It does not think about service layer patterns so you can swap CMS providers later without rewriting your frontend. Code splitting strategies, caching layers, how your component library scales when someone adds 30 pages next quarter. None of that is in the prompt.

At Bell Canada, the work involved bilingual Next.js applications with headless CMS integrations deployed on AWS. That same architectural discipline applies to a five-page small business site built through Tatham Tech. The scale changes. The standards don't.

AI writes the bricks. I design the building.

## Content: AI Drafts, I Edit

Most clients need help with website copy. This is where AI has genuinely changed what smaller budgets can afford.

AI drafts initial copy based on the client's business, audience, and goals. Headlines, product descriptions, about page content, blog outlines. A reasonable first draft of all of it, fast.

Reasonable and good are not the same word. AI copy is bland, repetitive, and addicted to words like "leverage" and "innovative" and "solutions." It writes in a voice that belongs to nobody. Every brand sounds like the same corporate ghost.

Every piece of content gets edited for voice, accuracy, and specificity. The fluff gets cut. The copy ends up sounding like the actual business it represents, not like a template with the company name swapped in. And everything gets fact-checked, because AI will state something completely wrong with the confidence of a guy who is sure he has been to that restaurant before.

AI gets the draft to 60%. Editing gets it to 100%. The client gets better copy in less time. The project stays on budget.

## Testing: AI Catches What Humans Skip

Writing tests is one of those things developers know they should do more of and almost always cut when deadlines get tight. Every single time.

AI shifts that equation. Unit tests, integration tests, edge case scenarios. It is particularly good at thinking through boundary conditions a human might skip. What happens when the input is empty? A string with 10,000 characters? An API returning a 500 error on a Tuesday for no reason?

The test scaffolding and many of the cases come from AI. The review is mine. There is a meaningful difference between tests that pad a coverage report and tests that actually prevent regressions in production. Knowing that difference is the whole job, really.

## SEO: AI Analyzes, I Strategize

Search engine optimization involves a lot of pattern analysis and data processing. AI was practically built for this.

Keyword research, search intent analysis, meta descriptions, structured data markup. AI processes competitor analysis faster than any human, spotting gaps and opportunities in minutes. The raw analytical work is genuinely impressive.

SEO strategy is a different muscle. Which keywords are worth targeting given the client's domain authority? Can we realistically compete on this topic? Local search or broader terms? These calls require understanding the client's business context, their competition, and the current state of search. Data alone doesn't get you there.

AI provides better data to work with. The strategic calls are still human.

Fair question. Short answer: no.
