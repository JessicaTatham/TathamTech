I'm going to tell you what to look for when hiring a freelance web developer. And I'm going to tell you from the developer's side, which might seem like a conflict of interest but is actually the opposite. I know what separates good developers from bad ones because I've been doing this for over a decade. I know the shortcuts people take. I know the red flags. And I'd rather you hire the right person, even if that person isn't me, than end up stuck with a bad build and a worse experience.

So here is the buyer's guide.

## Look at Their Portfolio, but Look at It Right

Everyone says "check the portfolio." That's obvious. But most people don't know what to look for beyond "does it look nice?"

Here's what actually matters:

**Variety.** A portfolio that shows five nearly identical sites tells you the developer has one template they reskin. Look for range. Different industries, different layouts, different functionality. This tells you they can think, not just copy and paste.

**Live sites.** Click through to the actual websites. A screenshot in a portfolio is meaningless. The real site might be slow, broken, or offline. If you can't visit it, ask why.

**Performance.** While you're on those live sites, run them through Google PageSpeed Insights. A developer's portfolio sites should load fast. If they don't care about performance on the work they show off, they won't care about it on yours.

**Mobile experience.** Pull up the portfolio sites on your phone. Do they work well? Is the text readable? Are the buttons easy to tap? Mobile is where most of your visitors are. If the developer's showcase sites aren't mobile-friendly, that tells you everything.

**Recency.** When were these sites built? A portfolio full of work from 2020 doesn't tell you much about what the developer can do with today's tools and standards.

## Communication Style Matters More Than You Think

You're going to work with this person for weeks or months. How they communicate during the sales process is a preview of how they'll communicate during the project.

Pay attention to:

**Response time.** Not expecting instant replies. But if it takes them four days to respond to your initial inquiry, that's how the whole project will go.

**Clarity.** Do they explain things in plain language? Or do they hide behind jargon? A developer who can't explain their process to a non-technical person either doesn't understand it well enough or doesn't respect your time enough. Both are problems.

**Questions.** A good developer asks questions before giving you a quote. Lots of questions. About your business, your goals, your audience, your timeline, your budget. If someone quotes you a price after a five-minute conversation, they're guessing. And you'll pay for that guess later in change orders and scope creep.

**Directness.** The best developers will push back on bad ideas. Gently, but clearly. If you say "I want a homepage with six rotating sliders and auto-playing music," a good developer will tell you why that's a bad idea. A bad developer will just bill you for it.

## Do They Explain or Just Do?

This is a big one. Some developers treat clients like they're just signing checks. They build whatever they want, hand it over, and disappear. You end up with a site you don't understand, can't update, and can't explain to anyone.

A good developer walks you through decisions. Why they chose this platform. Why the navigation is structured this way. Why that feature you wanted isn't worth the trade-off. Not because they're showing off, but because you should understand what you're paying for.

When you're talking to potential developers, ask them to explain something technical. It doesn't matter what. How hosting works. Why site speed matters. What a content management system does. Listen to how they explain it. If they make you feel stupid, move on. If they make you feel informed, that's a good sign.

## Who Owns the Code?

This is non-negotiable. When the project is done, you need to own everything. The code. The domain. The hosting account. The content. Everything.

Ask this question directly: "When the project is complete, do I own the code and all the assets?"

The answer should be an immediate and unequivocal yes.

Some developers build sites on their own hosting accounts or their own platforms. Some use proprietary tools that lock you into working with them forever. This isn't always malicious. Sometimes it's just laziness. But the effect is the same: you're trapped.

Here's what you should own and control:

- Domain name (registered in your name with your login)
- Hosting account (in your name)
- Code repository (or at minimum, a complete copy of the source code)
- CMS admin access
- All content, images, and assets
- Analytics access (Google Analytics, Search Console)

If a developer won't agree to this, they're not protecting their work. They're protecting their leverage.

## What Happens After Launch?

A website isn't a painting you hang on the wall and forget about. It needs updates. Security patches. Content changes. Bug fixes. Performance monitoring.

Ask every developer you talk to: "What happens after the site launches?"

Good answers include:

- "I offer a maintenance plan for ongoing updates and monitoring."
- "I'll train you on the CMS so you can make basic updates yourself."
- "I'm available for one-off requests on an hourly basis after launch."
- "Here's my standard post-launch support period and what it includes."

Bad answers include:

- Silence.
- "We can talk about that later."
- "I build it and then it's your responsibility."

Your site will need attention after launch. Know who is providing it and what it costs before you sign anything.

## Red Flags

Here are the things that should make you pause or walk away entirely.

**No contract.** A professional freelancer has a contract. It covers scope, timeline, payment terms, ownership, revisions, and what happens if things go sideways. No contract means no protection for either of you. This is amateur hour.

**No timeline.** "It'll take a few weeks" isn't a timeline. You should get milestones. Design review by this date. Development complete by this date. Launch by this date. Flexibility is fine. Vagueness isn't.

**They won't show previous work.** NDAs are real and some work genuinely can't be shared. But if a developer can't show you anything they've built, that's a problem. Even NDA-restricted work can be described in general terms.

**They quote without understanding scope.** If someone gives you a firm price before understanding what you need, they're either going to come back with change orders later or they're going to cut corners to hit their number. Neither is good.

**They bad-mouth other developers.** The tech world is full of people who think everyone else's code is garbage. A developer who spends your initial call criticizing your current site or previous developer is telling you more about themselves than about the work.

**No questions about your business.** If the developer doesn't ask about your audience, your goals, your competitors, or your content, they're building a website in a vacuum. A website is a business tool. If the developer doesn't understand the business, the tool won't work.

**They promise everything.** "Absolutely, we can do that" to every request is a yellow flag. Good developers set expectations. They tell you what is realistic within your budget and timeline. A yes-person will over-promise and under-deliver.

## The Contract Checklist

When you get to the contract stage, make sure it covers:

1. Detailed scope of work (what's included and what's not)
2. Timeline with milestones
3. Payment schedule (never pay 100% upfront, a common split is 50% to start and 50% at launch)
4. Revision policy (how many rounds, what counts as a revision)
5. Ownership clause (you own everything upon final payment)
6. Cancellation terms (what happens if either party needs to walk away)
7. Post-launch support terms

If any of these are missing, ask about them. If the developer resists putting them in writing, find a different developer.

## Trust Your Gut

After all the practical advice, there's one more thing. Pay attention to how you feel during the initial conversations. Do you feel heard? Do you feel respected? Do you feel like this person is genuinely interested in helping your business, or are you just a line item?

The best client-developer relationships are built on mutual respect and clear communication. You should feel confident that this person understands what you need and can deliver it. If something feels off, it probably is.

Take your time. Ask your questions. Check the references. Read the contract. Then make a decision you feel good about.
