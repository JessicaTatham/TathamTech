# What an AI Consultant Actually Does for a Small Business (And Whether You Need One)

The phrase "AI consultant" conjures up a very specific image. Someone in a suit, probably at McKinsey, charging six figures to produce a slide deck with the word "synergy" on every other page. An engagement that lasts nine months and ends with recommendations nobody implements.

That's enterprise consulting. It exists. It costs what a house costs. And it has absolutely nothing to do with what AI consulting looks like for a five-person accounting firm or a local e-commerce brand.

I do AI consulting work for small businesses, so I have some opinions about what it actually involves, what it costs, and when you genuinely don't need it. This isn't a sales pitch. Sometimes the answer really is "just use ChatGPT and you're fine."

## What the Work Actually Looks Like

For a small business, AI consulting breaks into a few concrete activities. None of them are mysterious.

### The Audit (Figuring Out What's Worth Automating)

This is usually where things start. You sit down with someone who understands both AI capabilities and business operations, and you map out where time is being wasted.

Not everything that can be automated should be. The question isn't "can AI do this?" because in 2026, the answer to that is almost always yes. The question is whether the time and cost of setting it up pays for itself within a reasonable timeframe. A task you do for ten minutes once a month? Probably not worth building an automation around. A task that eats two hours every day? That's where it gets interesting.

The audit typically produces a prioritized list. High-impact, low-effort items go first. The output is practical: these specific workflows, automated with these specific tools, saving this many hours per week. Not a philosophy document about "digital transformation."

### Tool Selection

The AI tool market is genuinely overwhelming right now. There are thousands of options, they all claim to do everything, and half of them will be dead in a year. Picking the right tools without wasting money on the wrong ones is a real problem.

A consultant who works with small businesses should know which tools actually deliver at the price points that make sense for you. That means knowing the difference between a $20/month tool that does 80% of what you need and a $200/month tool that does 85%. It means knowing which free tiers are genuinely useful and which ones are bait.

I wrote a whole piece on the [tools I actually recommend to clients](/blog/ai-tools-i-recommend-to-clients), if you want the specifics. The short version: you probably need fewer tools than you think, and the ones you need are probably cheaper than you expect.

### Implementation and Setup

This is where the biggest gap exists between types of consultants. Some people will audit your business, recommend tools, write up a nice PDF, and leave. You're then on your own to actually make it all work.

That approach is fine if you're technical. It's useless if you're not.

The implementation side means actually setting things up. Connecting your CRM to your email system through a workflow tool. Building the prompts that generate useful output instead of generic garbage. Configuring automations so they trigger at the right time, with the right data, and produce results that don't require you to redo them manually.

I covered some of these workflows in my post on [AI automations for small businesses](/blog/ai-automation-workflows-small-business). The point is that "set up AI" can mean anything from signing up for a ChatGPT subscription to building a multi-step automation pipeline that touches five different systems. A consultant handles the latter.

### Training Your Team

This one gets overlooked constantly. You can build the perfect automation, pick the ideal tools, wire everything together beautifully. If your team doesn't know how to use it, or doesn't trust it, or quietly goes back to the old way of doing things within two weeks, you've wasted your money.

Good training isn't a one-hour Zoom call where someone reads off slides. It's hands-on, using the actual tools with actual business data, answering the questions that come up when people try to do their real jobs with new systems. It usually takes a few sessions over a couple of weeks, because questions surface after people have actually tried working differently.

## When You Don't Need a Consultant

I'm going to be honest about this because it matters. Plenty of small businesses don't need to hire someone.

**You just need a chatbot for general tasks.** If your use case is "I want to use AI to help me write emails and brainstorm ideas," you need a ChatGPT or Claude subscription. That's $20/month. You don't need to pay someone to tell you that.

**You're already technical.** If you're comfortable with tools like Zapier or Make, you can read API documentation without your eyes glazing over, and you have the time to experiment, you can probably figure out your own automations. There are enough tutorials and guides online (including [this one on how AI helps small businesses](/blog/how-ai-can-help-your-small-business)) to get you very far on your own.

**Your needs are simple and well-defined.** You want to auto-respond to form submissions. You want AI to draft social media posts. These are well-documented use cases with straightforward implementations. A few hours of research and experimentation will get you there.

**You're not actually ready.** This is the uncomfortable one. If your business processes aren't defined, if you don't know where your time goes, if your existing tools are a mess, AI consulting won't fix that. You might need operational help first. Adding AI on top of chaos produces faster chaos.

## When You Probably Do Need One

**You're spending money on AI tools that aren't delivering.** I talk to business owners who are paying for four or five different AI subscriptions and barely using any of them. Or using them in ways that don't save meaningful time. An outside perspective can cut through the sunk cost and reorganize things.

**You need custom integrations.** Your booking system needs to talk to your CRM, which needs to trigger follow-up emails based on AI-generated content personalized to each client. That's not a "sign up for a tool" situation. That requires someone who understands APIs, workflow automation, and how to make different systems work together reliably.

**You want AI woven into your actual workflows, not bolted on as a side thing.** There's a difference between "we use ChatGPT sometimes" and "AI handles our lead qualification, content drafting, scheduling, and reporting as integrated parts of how we operate." The second one requires thinking about your business as a system and designing AI into it properly.

**You've tried and gotten stuck.** Maybe you started building automations and hit a wall. Maybe things work inconsistently. Maybe you can't figure out why the AI output is mediocre despite what the marketing promised. Someone who's solved these problems dozens of times can usually unstick you in a fraction of the time it would take to figure it out alone.

**Time is the real constraint.** You know you could probably figure this out eventually. But "eventually" keeps getting pushed back because you're running a business. If the opportunity cost of your time is high enough, paying someone to compress months of experimentation into a week or two makes financial sense.

## What It Actually Costs

Enterprise AI consulting starts at $50,000 and goes up from there. That's irrelevant to you.

For a small business engagement, realistic numbers look like this:

- **AI audit only** (assessment and recommendations): $500 to $1,500. You get a clear picture of opportunities, prioritized by impact. You handle implementation yourself.
- **Audit plus implementation**: $1,500 to $3,500. Someone figures out what to do and then actually does it. This is the most common engagement I see.
- **Full setup with training**: $2,500 to $5,000. The whole package: audit, tool selection, implementation, and making sure your team can actually use everything.
- **Ongoing support**: Usually a monthly retainer, $200 to $500/month for adjustments, new automations, and troubleshooting as needs evolve.

These numbers assume a small business. Five to fifty employees, straightforward tools, reasonable scope. If you're hearing numbers significantly above this range for similar scope, someone is charging enterprise rates for small business work.

The ROI math is usually straightforward. If an engagement costs $3,000 and saves 10 hours per week across your team at a blended cost of $40/hour, that's $400/week in recovered time. Payback period is about two months. Everything after that is pure upside.

## The PDF Problem

I mentioned this earlier but it deserves its own section. There's a real divide in this field between people who do the work and people who write about the work.

A "consultant" who delivers a 30-page strategy document and bills you $4,000 for it has given you homework. You now have to figure out how to execute on their recommendations, troubleshoot problems they didn't anticipate, and adapt when things don't work as described. If you had the expertise to do all that, you probably didn't need the document in the first place.

When evaluating someone for this kind of work, ask directly: do you implement, or do you advise? Both have value in the right context, but they're different services at different price points, and you should know which one you're buying.

## Finding the Right Person

A few things to look for:

**They ask about your business before they talk about AI.** If someone leads with technology rather than trying to understand what you actually do, your problems, your workflows, they're going to recommend solutions to problems you don't have.

**They can show you examples.** Not case studies from Fortune 500 companies. Examples relevant to your size and type of business.

**They're honest about limitations.** AI can't do everything well. Anyone who tells you otherwise is selling something. The good ones will tell you where AI isn't the answer.

**They work at your scale.** Someone who usually serves enterprise clients will over-engineer solutions for a small business. You want someone who understands that "good enough and running today" beats "perfect and deployed in six months."

## The Bottom Line

AI consulting for a small business is a practical service, not a mystical one. Someone looks at how you work, identifies where AI tools can save real time or money, sets things up properly, and makes sure your team can use them. The cost is measured in thousands, not tens of thousands.

Whether you need it depends on your technical comfort level, the complexity of what you're trying to do, and whether your time is better spent running your business than learning to configure workflow automation tools.
