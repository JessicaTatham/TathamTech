I get asked about AI tools constantly. Clients see me using them, read about them everywhere, and want to know which ones are worth paying for. The answer depends entirely on what your business actually does, but after two years of testing these things professionally and recommending them to real people with real budgets, I have opinions.

Some of these tools I use daily. Some I recommend to specific types of clients. A few I actively steer people away from. I'm not going to pretend they're all equally good or that any of them will transform your business overnight. But the right combination, set up properly, can genuinely save you 5-10 hours a week.

If you want the bigger picture on how AI fits into a small business, I wrote about that [here](/blog/how-ai-can-help-your-small-business). This post is about specific tools and whether they deserve your money.

## ChatGPT (OpenAI)

**What it does:** General-purpose AI assistant. Writing, brainstorming, research, analysis, code, customer email drafts, summarizing documents. It does a lot of things reasonably well.

**Who it's for:** Everyone, honestly. If you're only going to pay for one AI tool, this is probably it.

**Cost:** Free tier available. Plus is $20/month. Team plan is $25/user/month.

**My take:** ChatGPT is the Swiss Army knife. It's not the best at any single task, but it's good enough at most of them that it earns its spot. I use it for quick research, drafting client emails when I don't want to think about tone, and generating first passes at content. The Plus plan is worth it for the faster responses and GPT-4o access alone.

The downside is that it can be confidently wrong, especially with anything technical or niche. You still need to verify its output. And the free tier is fine for casual use but frustrating if you're trying to get real work done during peak hours.

## Claude (Anthropic)

**What it does:** Similar to ChatGPT but with notably better performance on long documents, nuanced writing, and complex analysis. Also significantly better at following detailed instructions.

**Who it's for:** Anyone who writes a lot, works with long documents, or needs an AI that actually listens when you give it specific guidelines.

**Cost:** Free tier available. Pro is $20/month. Team is $30/user/month.

**My take:** I use Claude more than ChatGPT these days, particularly for anything involving writing or working with large amounts of text. If you give Claude a style guide and tell it to avoid certain patterns, it actually does. ChatGPT tends to nod along and then do whatever it wants anyway.

For coding and development work, Claude is my primary tool. For small business owners who aren't writing code, the main advantage is the writing quality and the ability to upload long documents and get genuinely useful summaries or analysis back.

The downside is that Claude doesn't have the plugin ecosystem that ChatGPT has, and it can't browse the web on its own (though this is changing). If you need real-time information, ChatGPT still wins there.

## Notion AI

**What it does:** AI built directly into Notion's workspace. Summarizes pages, generates content, answers questions about your existing documents, fills databases, and automates repetitive note-taking.

**Who it's for:** Businesses already using Notion for project management, documentation, or wikis. Not worth switching to Notion just for the AI features.

**Cost:** $10/user/month on top of your existing Notion plan.

**My take:** If you're already in Notion, this is a no-brainer add-on. The ability to ask questions about your own workspace is genuinely useful. "Summarize last week's meeting notes" or "What did we decide about the pricing change?" actually works when your data is already in Notion.

If you're not already a Notion user, this isn't a reason to start. The AI features are good but they're an enhancement to an existing workflow, not a standalone product. I recommend it to about half my clients because about half my clients already use Notion.

## Canva Magic Studio

**What it does:** AI-powered design tools inside Canva. Background removal, image generation, magic resize, text-to-image, brand-consistent template generation. It handles a lot of the tedious design work that used to require opening Photoshop.

**Who it's for:** Small businesses that create their own marketing materials, social media graphics, presentations, or ads. Which is most small businesses.

**Cost:** Included in Canva Pro at $13/month (or $120/year). Free tier has limited AI features.

**My take:** This is one of my strongest recommendations for small business clients. Most of them are already using Canva, and the AI features meaningfully speed up the design process. Background removal alone used to cost money as a separate tool. Magic resize saves an absurd amount of time when you need the same graphic in six different dimensions.

The image generation is fine for social media filler but won't replace a professional designer for anything important. The real value is in the automation of repetitive design tasks, not the generative stuff.

## Zapier

**What it does:** Connects your apps together with automated workflows. When X happens in one app, do Y in another app. With AI steps, those workflows can now include intelligent decision-making and content generation.

**Who it's for:** Any business that uses multiple SaaS tools and is tired of manually moving data between them.

**Cost:** Free tier (limited). Starter at $20/month. Professional at $49/month. The AI features are available on paid plans.

**My take:** Zapier is the tool I recommend most often to clients because it solves a universal problem: you're copying data between apps manually and hating it. The AI integration means your automations can now do things like categorize incoming emails, draft personalized responses, or summarize form submissions before routing them.

I wrote a whole post about [specific automation workflows](/blog/ai-automation-workflows-small-business) if you want the details. The short version is that Zapier with AI steps can replace a surprising amount of administrative busywork.

The downside is that it gets expensive fast. Once you have 20+ workflows running, you're looking at $49-99/month. For businesses doing enough volume to need that many automations, it's still worth it. But keep an eye on the bill.

## Make (formerly Integromat)

**What it does:** Same general category as Zapier. App automation and workflow building. More visual, more flexible, better for complex multi-step workflows. Slightly steeper learning curve.

**Who it's for:** Businesses with more complex automation needs, or anyone who finds Zapier's pricing aggressive (which is everyone eventually).

**Cost:** Free tier available. Core at $9/month. Pro at $16/month. Significantly cheaper than Zapier for comparable usage.

**My take:** I tend to recommend Make over Zapier for clients who are more technically comfortable or who need complex workflows with branching logic. The visual builder is genuinely excellent and the pricing is much more reasonable at scale.

The trade-off is a steeper learning curve and a slightly smaller app library. If you just need "when someone fills out my form, send me a Slack notification," Zapier is simpler. If you need "when someone fills out my form, check their email against my CRM, route them to different workflows based on their company size, and generate a personalized follow-up," Make handles that more elegantly.

## Tidio

**What it does:** AI-powered customer service chatbot for websites. Handles common questions, routes complex issues to humans, collects leads, and operates 24/7.

**Who it's for:** E-commerce businesses, service businesses with repetitive customer questions, anyone who gets the same ten support emails every day.

**Cost:** Free tier (limited). Starter at $29/month. The AI chatbot features start at the Communicator plan ($25/month) plus the AI add-on.

**My take:** I recommend Tidio specifically for businesses that get a high volume of repetitive questions. "What are your hours?" "Do you ship to Canada?" "How do I return something?" If you're answering the same questions daily, an AI chatbot trained on your FAQ is an obvious win.

The setup takes a couple of hours to do properly. You need to feed it your actual FAQ content and test it thoroughly before letting it loose on real customers. A chatbot that gives wrong answers is worse than no chatbot at all.

I used to recommend Intercom for this, but their pricing has gotten out of control for small businesses. Tidio gives you 80% of the functionality at a quarter of the cost.

## Grammarly

**What it does:** Writing assistance. Grammar, spelling, tone, clarity. The AI features now include rewriting, tone adjustment, and full text generation.

**Who it's for:** Anyone who writes customer-facing content and doesn't have an editor.

**Cost:** Free tier (basic grammar). Premium at $12/month. Business at $15/user/month.

**My take:** Grammarly is useful but increasingly redundant if you're already paying for ChatGPT or Claude. I still recommend it for clients who write a lot of emails and want inline correction without switching to a separate tool. The browser extension catching typos in real-time is genuinely handy.

But if someone asks me "should I pay for both Grammarly Premium and ChatGPT Plus?" the answer is usually no. Pick one. ChatGPT or Claude can do everything Grammarly does and more. Grammarly's advantage is that it's passive and always on, which matters for some people's workflows.

## Tools I Don't Recommend (for Small Business)

**Jasper:** Overpriced for what it does. It was impressive in 2023 before ChatGPT got good at the same tasks. At $49/month for the Creator plan, you're paying a premium for templates and a marketing-specific wrapper around the same underlying AI models. ChatGPT Plus at $20/month does the same work with more flexibility. I can't justify recommending Jasper to a small business client anymore.

**Copy.ai:** Similar story to Jasper but cheaper. It's fine. It's just not better than ChatGPT for most use cases, and adding another subscription to your stack for marginal benefit isn't great advice.

**Generic "AI writing tools" with no clear differentiation:** There are dozens of these now. Most are thin wrappers around OpenAI's API with a markup. If their main selling point is "we write blog posts for you," save your money. A well-crafted prompt in ChatGPT or Claude will give you the same output without the monthly fee.

**Midjourney for most small businesses:** Incredible tool, genuinely. But unless your business specifically needs custom AI-generated imagery on a regular basis, the $10-30/month doesn't justify itself. Canva's built-in generation handles the occasional social media graphic fine.

## How to Pick What's Right for You

Start with one or two tools. Seriously. The worst thing you can do is sign up for seven AI subscriptions, use each of them once, and then forget about them while they quietly drain your credit card.

My usual recommendation for most small businesses:

1. **ChatGPT Plus or Claude Pro** (pick one), $20/month
2. **Canva Pro** if you make your own graphics, $13/month
3. **Zapier or Make** if you're drowning in repetitive admin work, $20-50/month

That covers writing, design, and automation for under $100/month. Add Tidio if customer support volume justifies it. Add Notion AI if you're already in Notion.

Give each tool at least two weeks of genuine daily use before deciding it's not for you. Most of the value comes after you've figured out how to integrate it into your actual workflow, not from the first time you open it and type "write me a blog post."
