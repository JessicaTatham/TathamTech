You built a website. You paid for it. It exists. But when you Google your business, it's nowhere. When you search for what your business does plus your city, still nothing. You start to wonder if the internet is broken.

It's not broken. Your site has a problem. Probably more than one.

I audit small business websites regularly, and the same issues come up over and over. The good news is that most of them are fixable. The less good news is that someone should have handled these when the site was built. But here we are.

Let me walk through the most common reasons and what to do about each one.

## You Haven't Submitted Your Site to Google Search Console

This is the number one issue I see. People assume that once you publish a website, Google automatically knows about it. That's technically true, eventually. Google's crawlers will find your site on their own. But "eventually" can mean weeks or months, and there's no guarantee they'll crawl all your pages.

Google Search Console is a free tool that lets you tell Google your site exists. You can submit your sitemap, see how Google views your pages, check for crawl errors, and monitor your search performance.

**The fix:** Go to search.google.com/search-console. Verify your domain (Google will walk you through it). Submit your sitemap. This takes about 15 minutes and it's the single most impactful thing you can do.

## You Don't Have a Sitemap

A sitemap is a file that lists every page on your website. It's like handing Google a map of your site instead of making them wander around and find everything themselves.

Most modern website platforms generate sitemaps automatically. But "most" isn't "all," and "automatically" doesn't mean "correctly." I've seen sites where the sitemap existed but wasn't accessible, wasn't updated when new pages were added, or pointed to pages that no longer existed.

**The fix:** Check if your site has a sitemap by going to yourdomain.com/sitemap.xml. If it loads and shows your pages, good. Submit it to Google Search Console if you haven't already. If it doesn't exist or is empty, your website platform should have a setting or plugin to generate one. WordPress users can use Yoast SEO or Rank Math. Most modern frameworks generate them with minimal configuration.

## Your Pages Have Thin Content

Google doesn't want to show people pages that have barely any information. If your service pages have two sentences and a stock photo, Google has no reason to rank them. There's not enough content for Google to understand what the page is about or to consider it valuable to searchers.

This is one of the most common issues I see. Business owners create pages for each service but only write a headline and a sentence or two. From their perspective, the important thing is that the page exists. From Google's perspective, the page is empty.

**The fix:** Every important page on your site should have at least 300 words of useful, original content. Not filler. Not keyword-stuffed nonsense. Actual information that a potential customer would find helpful. Describe what the service is, who it's for, what the process looks like, what makes your approach different. Write like you're explaining it to someone who just asked you about it at a networking event.

## You Have No Meta Descriptions

A meta description is the snippet of text that appears below your page title in Google search results. It doesn't directly affect ranking, but it affects whether people click on your result. And click-through rate does affect ranking.

More importantly, missing meta descriptions signal to Google that the page was not set up with care. Google will generate its own description from your page content, and it usually picks something random and unhelpful.

**The fix:** Write a unique meta description for every page. Keep it under 160 characters. Make it a clear, compelling summary of what the page is about. Include your primary keyword naturally. Every page. Not just the homepage.

## Your Title Tags Are Duplicate or Generic

The title tag is the clickable headline in search results. It's one of the most important on-page SEO signals. If every page on your site has the same title tag (or worse, the default "Home" or "Page 1"), Google can't differentiate your pages.

I see this constantly. Five pages, all with the title "My Business Name." Google looks at that and doesn't know which page to show for which search.

**The fix:** Every page needs a unique title tag that includes a relevant keyword and your business name. Format: "Primary Keyword - Business Name." For example: "Emergency Plumbing Repair in Austin - Smith Plumbing." Keep it under 60 characters. Make every single one unique.

## You Have No Backlinks

Backlinks are links from other websites to yours. They're one of the most important ranking factors in Google's algorithm. A brand new site with no backlinks is like a new restaurant with no reviews. Google has no external signal that your site is trustworthy or relevant.

This is the hardest problem to fix because you can't fully control it. But you can influence it.

**The fix:** Start with the easy wins. Get listed on Google Business Profile (this is free and critical for local businesses). Get listed on relevant directories like Yelp, industry-specific directories, and your local chamber of commerce. If you're a member of professional organizations, make sure they link to your site. Ask vendors and partners for links. Create content worth linking to, like genuinely helpful blog posts, local guides, or resources.

Backlink building is a long game. But if you're doing nothing, you're falling behind.

## Your Site Is Too New

If your site launched last week, it won't rank yet. Google needs time to crawl, index, and evaluate your site. This is normal. It typically takes two to six months for a new site to start appearing in search results for competitive terms.

**The fix:** Be patient, but don't be passive. Submit your sitemap to Search Console. Create content regularly. Build backlinks. The sites that rank fastest are the ones that do the other things on this list right from day one.

## Your Platform Is Blocking Indexing

Some website builders and templates accidentally (or intentionally) block search engines from indexing the site. This is usually done with a robots.txt file or a meta tag that tells Google not to crawl the page.

This sounds unlikely but it happens more than you'd think. I've seen staging sites go live with the "noindex" tag still in place. I've seen developers turn off indexing during development and forget to turn it back on.

**The fix:** Check your robots.txt file at yourdomain.com/robots.txt. It shouldn't say "Disallow: /" (that blocks everything). Then view the source code of your pages and look for a meta tag that says "noindex." If you find either of these, fix them immediately. If you don't know how, ask your developer. This is urgent because every day your site is blocked is a day it doesn't exist as far as Google is concerned.

## Your Site Isn't Mobile-Friendly

Google uses mobile-first indexing. That means Google primarily uses the mobile version of your site to determine rankings. If your site doesn't work well on mobile, Google will rank it lower. Period.

**The fix:** Test your site with Google's mobile-friendly test tool. Fix the issues it identifies. If the problems are structural (the site wasn't built to be responsive), this might require a redesign rather than a patch.

## The Audit I Do for Every Client

When I audit a client's site, I check every one of these items. Without fail, most small business sites have at least three or four of these problems. Some have all of them.

The patterns are remarkably consistent. The developer built the site, made it look nice, but didn't set up the SEO fundamentals. Or the business owner built it themselves on a template and didn't know these things mattered. Nobody's at fault, really. These aren't obvious unless you know to look for them.

But they are the difference between a website that works for your business and a website that just sits there looking pretty while nobody finds it.

## Where to Start

If you're overwhelmed, do these three things first:

1. Set up Google Search Console and submit your sitemap. This is free and takes 15 minutes.
2. Write unique title tags and meta descriptions for every page. Budget an hour for this.
3. Check for robots.txt blocks and noindex tags. Five minutes to check, five minutes to fix.

Those three things alone will move the needle. Then work through the rest of the list as you have time.

Google isn't mysterious. It has rules. Follow them, and your site will show up. Ignore them, and it won't. It's that straightforward.
